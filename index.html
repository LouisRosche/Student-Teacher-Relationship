Your Guide to Building Strong Teacher Relationships

Actions That Lead to Your Success

Why This Matters to YOU

Research tracking over 2.6 million students across 70+ years shows that students who have positive relationships with their teachers are significantly more engaged in learning and achieve better grades. The effect is so strong that researchers measured it at 0.72 on the effect size scale—that's considered highly significant. Translation: How you interact with your teachers directly impacts your learning and grades.

Bottom Line: Students who actively build positive relationships with teachers learn more, participate more, and perform better. This guide shows you exactly HOW to do that with specific, observable behaviors you can start TODAY.

✓ DO: Actions That Build Strong Relationships
Engaging with Teachers
Greet Your Teacher
Make eye contact and say "hi" or "good morning" when you enter the classroom.
Looks like: Walking in, making eye contact with teacher at door, saying "Good morning, Ms. Chen" or just "Hey" with a nod.
Ask Questions Early
Raise your hand or use the agreed signal when you don't understand—within 5 minutes of instruction, not after class.
Looks like: Teacher explains a math problem, you're confused on step 2, you raise your hand right then and say "Can you explain step 2 again?"
Answer When Called On
Even if you're not sure, respond with something like "I'm not sure, but I think..." or "Can you rephrase the question?"
Looks like: Teacher asks you a question, you say "I'm not totally sure, but I think it has to do with the main character's motivation?" instead of staying silent or saying "I don't know."
Be Specific About Struggles
Say "I'm struggling with X" specifically rather than "I don't get it" or staying silent.
Looks like: "I understand how to set up the equation, but I'm stuck on how to solve for x when there are variables on both sides" NOT "I don't get this."
Try First, Then Ask
Attempt the problem or task for at least 2 minutes before asking for help. Show your thinking.
Looks like: Working on the problem, showing your steps even if wrong, then raising your hand and pointing to where you got stuck. "I tried doing this, but then I got stuck here."
Building Trust
Communicate About Deadlines Early
If you won't finish homework, tell your teacher BEFORE it's due. Ask for what you need.
Looks like: On Tuesday you realize you can't finish the essay due Friday because you have 3 tests. You tell your teacher Tuesday or Wednesday: "I have 3 tests this week. Can I turn the essay in Monday instead?"
Give Real Answers Sometimes
When teacher asks "How are you?", give an actual answer occasionally—not always just "fine."
Looks like: "Actually kind of stressed about the game tonight" or "Pretty good, excited about the field trip" or "Tired, stayed up late studying."
Accept Help When Offered
If teacher offers help time or tutoring, attend at least once in the first month.
Looks like: Teacher says "I have office hours Tuesday and Thursday after school." You show up at least once, even if just for 10 minutes to ask one question.
Read Feedback
Actually read teacher feedback on assignments before filing or throwing them away.
Looks like: Getting your graded essay back and reading the comments in the margins and at the end before putting it in your folder. Consider what the teacher wrote.
Classroom Citizenship
Say Thank You
Thank your teacher when they help you individually or return graded work.
Looks like: Teacher explains something one-on-one at your desk, you say "Thanks, that helps" or "Thank you" when they hand back your test.
Be Ready Quickly
Have materials out and be ready within 2 minutes of the bell ringing.
Looks like: Bell rings. Within 2 minutes your notebook, pencil, and textbook are on your desk. You're looking at the board for instructions, not still digging in your backpack.
Participate Weekly
Make at least one classroom contribution per week (answer, question, or comment).
Looks like: During discussion, you raise your hand and add one comment or answer one question voluntarily at some point during the week. Doesn't have to be every day.
Use Names
Use your teacher's name when addressing them respectfully.
Looks like: "Ms. Rodriguez, can you explain question 5?" or "Mr. Lee, I have a question about the reading" NOT just "Hey" or "Teacher."
✗ DON'T: Actions That Damage Relationships
Relationship Barriers
Don't Block Communication
Don't wear headphones/earbuds during instruction unless explicitly permitted by your teacher.
What this means: When teacher is talking to the class or giving instructions, earbuds are out. Music during independent work is only okay if teacher says it's okay.
Don't Turn Away
Don't turn your back, put your head down, or avoid eye contact when teacher addresses you individually.
What this means: If teacher walks over to help you or ask you a question, face them, look up, make eye contact even briefly. Don't put your hood up or turn away.
Don't Use Complete Silence
Don't refuse to speak at all. "I'd rather not answer" is acceptable, total silence isn't.
What this means: If teacher asks you a question and you really don't want to answer, say "Can you come back to me?" or "I'd rather not answer." Saying nothing and staring makes things worse.
Don't Start with Complaints
Don't enter class and immediately complain about the assignment or grading before even greeting the teacher.
What this means: Walking in and immediately saying "This homework was so stupid" or "Why did I get a bad grade?" sets a negative tone. Greet first, then discuss concerns calmly later.
Communication Mistakes
Don't Hide Struggles Then Blame
Don't say "I'm fine" when you're clearly struggling, then blame the teacher later for not helping you.
What this means: If teacher asks "Do you understand?" and you say "Yeah I'm good" when you're actually confused, you can't later say "You never helped me." Be honest when asked.
Don't Lie About Effort
Don't claim you "studied for hours" if you didn't. Be honest about your actual effort.
What this means: If you only studied 20 minutes and failed the test, don't tell teacher you studied for 3 hours. Say "I didn't study enough" or "I studied but I focused on the wrong things."
Don't Wait Until Failing
Don't wait until you have an F to speak up about not understanding material.
What this means: If you're confused in Week 2, speak up in Week 2. Don't wait until Week 8 when you're failing and then say "I never understood any of this."
Don't Interrupt
Don't interrupt the teacher mid-sentence. Raise your hand or wait for a pause.
What this means: Teacher is explaining something. You have a question. Don't shout it out while they're talking. Raise your hand and wait, or wait until they finish the explanation.
Counterproductive Behaviors
Don't Show Visible Frustration
Don't make disruptive noises (sighing, pen-clicking, talking to others) when teacher corrects your work.
What this means: Teacher marks something wrong on your paper. Don't sigh loudly, slam your pencil down, or mutter to your neighbor. If you're frustrated, talk to teacher privately after class.
Don't Mock Quietly
Don't mock the teacher's suggestions or instructions to classmates after the teacher walks away.
What this means: Teacher gives you feedback and walks away. Don't turn to your friend and whisper "That's so dumb" or roll your eyes. Teacher usually notices, and it damages trust.
Don't Refuse Reasonable Requests
Don't refuse to sit in an assigned seat or move when asked for a legitimate reason.
What this means: If teacher assigns seats or asks you to move because you're off-task, comply. You can discuss it later privately, but refusing in the moment creates conflict.
Don't Leave Without Acknowledging
Don't bolt out the door at the bell without any acknowledgment of the teacher.
What this means: Bell rings, you're packing up. Make brief eye contact with teacher, nod, or say "bye" or "see you tomorrow" as you leave. Don't just run out like teacher doesn't exist.
